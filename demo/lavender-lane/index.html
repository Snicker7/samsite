About the Farm

A third of an acre, a cutting garden, and a good pair of shears.

Lavender Lane is a small flower farm in northern Utah — a third of an acre of lavender, ranunculus, cosmos, dahlias, and whatever the season is willing to grow. It started as a cutting garden in 2021 and turned into a working farm the year after.

We take a small number of weddings each season. The rest of the year goes into a weekly flower subscription, a summer farm stand, and a handful of workshops that people drive up for. If it's in the bouquet, it was grown here or by a neighbor.

§ Ⅱ · De Horto

On the practice of growing and gathering.

This is a small operation — one farmer, two seasonal hands, a borrowed tiller, and a third of an acre that tries its best.

The field is planted with an intentional short list: Lavandula angustifolia for the lane itself; Dahlia pinnata for high summer; Cosmos bipinnatus for cutting; Ranunculus asiaticus for the brides in May. A little amaranth for trailing. A little bupleurum for filler. Nothing that can't earn its square foot.

Everything is cut the morning of delivery, conditioned on the porch, and wrapped in brown kraft. No floral foam ever. Compost goes back in the bed in October.

Lavender Harvest Begins in Earnest this Weekend, Farmer Reports

After a wet spring and a slow start, rows two and four have hit peak oil. The scissors are out. The lane smells like a postcard.

By The Farm · Reporting from the south lane

Lavender Lane Floral, a small cut-flower farm operating on a third of an acre north of Salt Lake City, will begin its third annual lavender harvest this Saturday morning, according to a statement released by the farmer over breakfast. Cutting begins at sunrise; the farm stand opens at nine.

The delay is owed to an unusually wet April, which pushed the peak-oil window back about ten days. Yields, however, are reported to be up nearly fifteen percent from last year's harvest, with the south lane producing especially dense heads of Grosso and Hidcote.

Wand-weaving workshops, a seasonal fixture that books out within an hour of announcement, will run every Saturday afternoon through the end of July. The farm asks that attendees wear closed-toe shoes and bring clippers if they have them.

Fresh lavender bundles will be available at the farm stand at $14, wands at $22, dried wreaths starting at $58, and a small number of bridal-scale installations by pre-order only.

Lavender Lane Floral is a one-farmer operation on a third of an acre in northern Utah. It started, like most small flower farms, as a cutting garden that got out of hand — three beds of ranunculus in 2020, a lavender row in 2021, and a farm stand by the summer of 2022.

The field supplies a weekly flower subscription, a Saturday farm stand (open June through September), a handful of seasonal workshops, and no more than twenty weddings a year. The rest of the year goes into composting, amendment, and planning the next season's cut list.

The rules here are few and strict. No floral foam. No imports. Every stem grown on this farm or, occasionally, from a neighbor in the same valley. If it's in the bouquet, it was cut this morning.
