Scene 02 · About

I'm Tucker. I make films and photos for couples who'd rather feel their wedding day than pose through it.

I started behind a video camera and never really put it down. Photography came after — but film is still the part I live for, the part I'm chasing more of every year. Long lenses from across the room. Quiet shots of you and your dad before the ceremony. The first three seconds of laughter after a vow. The stuff you'll forget in a week and want to relive in twenty years.

I work alone, I move quietly, and I'm probably the least intrusive vendor at your wedding. My job is to disappear into the day and bring it back to you on a screen.

Scene 05 · FAQ

Most asked, before you ask.

01

Do you travel for weddings?

Always. 2026 has me in California, San Diego, and England already, and I'm actively booking more. Travel is built into the elopement and out-of-state packages — no surprise fees.

02

Photo and video together — do I have to book both?

Yes. I shoot them as one job, which means the photos and the film tell the same story instead of two competing ones. It's also why my pricing is built around both being included.

03

How long until we see the film and photos?

A teaser within two weeks. Full gallery in 6–8 weeks. The feature film in 10–14 weeks — film takes longer because the music licensing and color pass aren't fast.

04

What if our day doesn't go to plan?

Mine never have either. I shoot documentary-style, which means I'm watching for the real moments instead of waiting for the planned ones. Rain, late vendors, a missing tie — those usually make the best frames.

Do you really travel for weddings?

Yes — and I'd rather book the whole year out of state than not. 2026 is California, San Diego, and England already, and I'm actively booking more. Travel is built into the destination package.

Can I book just photo or just film?

Not really — every package is both. I shoot them as one job, which is also why my pricing makes sense. Adding a separate videographer to a separate photographer almost always costs more.
